# Complex Numbers
A complex number is an entity which comes with two parts, it contains the real part and the imaginary part.
The imaginary part is considered the 'weird' part.
They are drawn as points within a plane, as shown in the following diagram.

Polar coordinates is a different description to define complex numbers. The polar coordinate consists of a non-negative real number (the absolute) and an angle known as the argument,

## Operations
Absolute: The absolute of a complex number, |𝑎 + 𝑏𝑖|, is given by:
$$ \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$$

Addition: In order to add the complex number you add the individual components, similar to vectors - you add the real components together and you add the imaginary components together.  

![[Pasted image 20240928152352.png#invert]]

The addition inverse of a complex number is that which you add to a complex number in order to obtain zero. 
The additive inverse of the complex number $$a + bi$$ is $$-a -bi $$ or $$- (a + bi) $$
 
Conjugate Complex: Compared to a complex number, it contains the same real part but the negative of the imaginary part.

![[Pasted image 20240928154807.png#invert]]

Multiplication: 
The multiplication operation on complex numbers is defined as: 
$$ (a + bi)(a' + b'i) = (aa' - bb') + (ab' + ba')i $$
It is difficult to comprehend the definition on its own, however, it is more easily grasped when looking at its absolute and argument (polar coordinates).

The product of the two complex numbers, is given by the product of the absolutes and the sum of the arguments:
$$ (a + bi)(a' + b'i) = (rr',φ+φ') $$

### Multiplication Properties:
To multiply a complex number, z, by another complex number, z', in order to get z, we use the multiplicative unit. The multiplicate unit comes from the number one but expressed as a complex number: $$1 = 1 + 0i $$
This makes z' the multiplicative inverse of z.

For every complex number that does not equal to zero, the multiplicative inverse is given by: $$ \frac{a}{a^2 + b^2} - \frac{b}{a^2 + b^2}i $$
