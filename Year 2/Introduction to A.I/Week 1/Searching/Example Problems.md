# Example Problems

A problem-solving approach has been applied to a vast array of task environments.
There are two kinds of problems used to test and develop AI.

## Standardised Problems

These are perfect, controlled environments for training an AI, they are designed to be simple to describe but can difficult to solve.
The main goal for this is to be a benchmark, researches can test different search algorithms on the same standardised problem and directly compare their performance, 

There are some examples of these in the book, one of which is the **8-Puzzle**:
- The 8-Puzzle involves moving the blank space Up, Down, Left or Right, and the goal is to get the numbers into a specific order. Its a perfect test because the rules are simple, but finding the shortest solution requires a smart algorithm.

## Real-World Problems

These are the complex, messy problems that people want to solve to get things done.
A characteristic of these problems are that they are **idiosyncratic** meaning *unique* as every real-world problem is slightly different.

An example of this would be robot navigation. Rather than following distinct paths, a robot can roam around making its own paths. When the robot has arms and legs that must also be controlled, the search-space becomes many-dimensional and so advanced techniques are required just to make the essentially continuous search space finite.
